Muslim Experiences in Canada / Recent Migration to Canada (Post 9/11)

After a century-long history of migration, the South Asian muslim communities constitute a visible and diverse presence in B.C today.

In particular, the emergence of diverse, often dissenting voices from within the community has complicated and enriched our understanding of South Asian Canadian muslims. On the one hand, minorities within minorities, double diaspora, second and third generation muslims, and queer muslims, among others, have challenged any one, uniform culture, history, and understanding of the community. Alongside, the enigma of reconciling one's Islamic upbringing with the seemingly “secular” and “liberal” values of Canadian society has also elicited different responses from different members of the community.

The emergence of particular narratives about Muslims in the aftermath of 9/11 has also affected the community's sense of itself.

Lastly, a number of recent ethnographic works also highlight the difficulties South Asian muslim migrants face in finding employment due to the lack of Canadian education and work experience.

Post 9/11

9/11 Marked a very difficult and challenging time for the Muslim communities of North America. They experienced increased surveillance, threat calls, and discrimination at both the state level and everyday social life in the aftermath of 9/11. The community members had to repeatedly prove themselves as “non-extremists”, and “non-threatening” in various social situations especially at airports.

Muslim Queerness

Itrath Syed, a former BCMA member, faced backlash from community members for her support of the same sex marriage during her election campaigns in 2004.[30] More recently, a number of community members participated in “Million March 4 Children” to protest against sexual orientation and Gender identity (SOGI) curriculum in schools.[31] These incidents indicate the precarity of queer South Asian muslims within the community.

With the legalization of same-sex marriage in 2005, organizations like Trikone Vancouver, Sher Vancouver, and Salaam Queer Muslim community emerged to offer support to queer South Asian communities in B.C. El-Farouk Khaki, Imtiaz Popat, and Fatima Jaffer are the three well known South Asian muslim queer activists.

Bengali

Recently, Bengali organizations in B.C have started regularly organizing events like West Coast Tagore Festival, International Mother language Festival, Pitha Utsav (festival of Bangladeshi pancakes, desserts), and Vaisakhi Mela (Bengali New Year celebration). These events are well- attended by Bengali community members of various religions, and nationalities, including Muslims.[32]

Sri Lanka

Sri Lanka's diverse Muslim community counts up to 9.7% of the total population. While the majority of the Muslims are Sunni, there are communities of those who follow Sufism. The beginnings of the Muslim population in Sri Lanka can be linked to the ancient trade paths connecting South and Southeast Asia with the Middle East. In the seventh century, traders from the Middle East (Arabs and Persians) established commercial connections in southern India that also extended to Sri Lanka. Middle Eastern traders wed Tamil and Sinhalese women and established roots in the eastern region of the island near Batticaloa and Ampara. Tuan Burhanuddin Jayah is seen as one of the national heroes of Sri Lanka and was the first Muslim cabinet minister in 1948.[33]

Ahmadiyya

Most of the Ahmadiyya community members in B.C consist of refugees and immigrants from Pakistan's Punjab region. A large number of community members have migrated to Canada to escape religious persecution especially after 1974 when they were declared non-muslims in Pakistan. Baitur Rehman Mosque was built in 2005 to fulfill the religious needs of the community. The community has also actively developed relationships with Indigenous communities of B.C.[34] Lastly, the community also organizes the annual World Religions Conference across B.C.[35]

Shia

As the recent work of Waged Jafer indicates, the historical narrative of Imam Hussain guides the self understanding of the Shia community in Vancouver. Amidst a predominantly Sunni Muslim presence in Canada, Shia community members often find themselves as a minority within a minority. Within this diasporic context, they actively “resist marginalization, seek equal recognition and equal participation” by mourning and remembering the story of Imam Hussain.[36]

Shia Muslim Community of British Columbia (SMCBC) is the largest organization of the community in B.C.[37] In 2002, the organization opened Az-Zahra Islamic Centre, which caters to the “spiritual, social, educational, and economic well being” of the community.

Solidarity with Indigenous communities

With an increased understanding of how the intertwined histories of colonialism, migration, and racism have affected South Asian muslim communities of B.C, a number of community members have also started developing relationships of solidarity and allyship with Indigenous communities of B.C.[38]
